## Field Pulse

This is our first roundup, so we are catching up on roughly six weeks of publications (early February through mid-March 2026). Fourteen papers hit the catalog today. Rather than walking through all of them, I will focus on the papers that actually shift how you should think about the field going forward.

**The headline result is Mitsui et al. in Angewandte** - a gold nanocluster (Au42(PET)32) sensitizing rubrene to reach 21.4% internal quantum yield at 808 nm and 15.0% at 936 nm excitation. Those numbers deserve scrutiny. The 808 nm result at 21.4% is exceptional but not unbelievable for rubrene, which has one of the most favorable spin-statistical factors of any annihilator (f ~ 0.58 in ideal conditions). What makes this noteworthy is that the sensitizer is a discrete gold nanocluster, not a porphyrin, not a QD, not an organometallic complex. Gold nanoclusters are atomically precise, synthetically tunable, and their triplet manifolds are only beginning to be understood. The 936 nm excitation at 15% QY is the number I keep coming back to - that is deep into the biological tissue transparency window, and if it holds up in independent labs, it opens bioimaging applications that were previously gated on sensitizer absorption.

**Two COF papers appeared nearly simultaneously** - Zhang et al. in Chem (Cell Press) and Brzezinski et al. in Angewandte, both demonstrating TTA-UC in covalent organic frameworks. This is not a coincidence. COFs have been the obvious next-generation host for solid-state TTA-UC since MOFs proved too heavy and too lossy. COFs give you crystalline order (controlled chromophore spacing), tunable pore chemistry, and structural rigidity, which is exactly what you need to suppress the parasitic triplet quenching pathways that plague amorphous polymer hosts. Zhang's contribution is particularly interesting because they demonstrate independent tunability of interface vs. bulk exciton dynamics within the COF, which gives you two separate design knobs.

**Jin et al. in ACS Catalysis** is the paper I would hand to a student asking "what should I work on next." An iron-based sensitizer for TTA-UC that overcomes the diffusion limit through preassociation of the sensitizer-annihilator pair. Earth-abundant metal sensitizers are the single biggest sustainability gap in TTA-UC. Almost everything we build uses palladium, platinum, ruthenium, or iridium - all rare, expensive, and supply-constrained. Iron is the fourth most abundant element in the Earth's crust. The preassociation strategy is clever because iron complexes typically have very short triplet lifetimes (ps to low ns), which makes diffusion-limited TTET essentially impossible. By locking the sensitizer and annihilator together, you bypass diffusion entirely. The question is whether this generalizes beyond the specific pair they demonstrate.

**Wang et al. in Nature Materials** describes proton-coupled triplet energy transfer from colloidal QDs to surface-anchored molecules. This is a new mechanism paper, not an efficiency record, but it matters because it tells us that the QD-to-molecule interface has pathways we were not accounting for. If proton shuttling mediates triplet transfer, then surface ligand chemistry and local pH become design variables for QD-sensitized upconversion - variables nobody was optimizing.

The Isokuortti and Nienhaus perspective in Chemical Science is worth reading cover to cover if you work on solid-state systems. Their proposal to unify TTA-UC, OLED, and singlet fission under a single excitonic framework is overdue. These three communities have been solving the same triplet management problems in parallel for a decade. The call for ML-driven phonon engineering in annihilator design is ambitious but directionally right.

## Industrial Lens

Three results here actually matter for someone building products:

**The gold nanocluster sensitizer (Mitsui)** is potentially transformative for bioimaging and biomedical applications. A 936 nm excitation source means you can use cheap, eye-safe diode lasers and get tissue penetration depths measured in centimeters, not millimeters. The catch: gold nanoclusters at the Au42 level are still research-grade materials. Synthesis scalability and batch-to-batch reproducibility are open questions that nobody has addressed yet.

**The COF papers (Zhang, Brzezinski)** represent the best current path to manufactureable solid-state TTA-UC devices. Unlike polymer matrices, COFs can in principle be produced as films, coatings, and membranes with defined thicknesses and chromophore densities. The "in principle" is doing a lot of work in that sentence - COF thin film processing at industrial scale is still an unsolved problem, but it is being actively attacked by the membranes and catalysis communities, so TTA-UC would benefit from their progress.

**The iron sensitizer (Jin)** matters for photocatalysis cost reduction. If you are building a solar fuel reactor, replacing your platinum porphyrin sensitizer with an iron complex is the difference between a research demo and a viable process. But the preassociation constraint is limiting - you need to pair this with annihilators that can be covalently linked without killing their photophysics.

The Hubner et al. paper on TTA-UC-driven aqueous radical polymerization is a nice application demo, but aqueous polymerization already has many light-driven routes. The value proposition of using upconverted photons here needs clearer articulation.

## Research Directions

Three directions stand out from this batch:

**1. Gold nanoclusters as a sensitizer class.** The Mitsui result is a single data point. We need systematic studies varying cluster size (Au25, Au38, Au42, Au102), ligand shell, and annihilator pairing. The triplet photophysics of atomically precise gold clusters are poorly mapped, and there may be sweet spots we have not found yet. Someone with access to both cluster synthesis and ultrafast spectroscopy could make a career out of this.

**2. COF-based solid-state TTA-UC devices.** Two high-impact papers in the same month tells you the field is converging here. The immediate bottleneck is oxygen tolerance - COFs are porous by design, which means oxygen diffuses right in and quenches your triplets. Someone needs to either develop self-healing oxygen scavenging within the COF pores or find COF topologies that are porous enough for chromophore loading but tortuous enough to limit O2 permeation. The zeolite approach from Kishimoto et al. in ChemComm is an interesting counterpoint, but zeolites bring their own problems (heavy, brittle, limited tunability).

**3. Earth-abundant sensitizer development beyond iron.** Jin's iron result is exciting but iron's ultrafast deactivation fundamentally limits what you can do. Copper(I) complexes with MLCT states, chromium(III) with its microsecond 2E lifetime, and even main-group sensitizers based on bismuth or antimony all deserve serious exploration. The preassociation strategy should be tested across multiple earth-abundant metals to see if it is a general solution or specific to the iron system studied.

One meta-observation: the field is clearly bifurcating into solution-phase mechanism studies and solid-state device development, and the gap between them is widening. Papers that bridge both - like the COF work that provides ordered structures amenable to both mechanistic study and device fabrication - will have outsized impact.
